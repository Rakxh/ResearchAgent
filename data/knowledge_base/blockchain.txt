Blockchain Technology

A blockchain is a distributed ledger that records transactions across many computers in a way that makes the recorded history difficult to alter retroactively without altering all subsequent blocks and gaining agreement from the network. Each block contains a cryptographic reference to the previous block, forming a chain, and changes to historical records require redoing the computational work for every subsequent block, which becomes prohibitively expensive as the chain grows. Beyond cryptocurrencies, blockchains have been explored for applications such as supply chain tracking, where an immutable record can help verify the provenance of goods, and for smart contracts, which are self-executing agreements encoded directly on the blockchain that automatically perform actions when predefined conditions are met. Scalability and energy consumption have been significant challenges for some blockchain designs, particularly those relying on proof-of-work consensus, which has driven interest in alternative consensus mechanisms such as proof-of-stake that require substantially less computational energy.
