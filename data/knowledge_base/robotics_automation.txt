Robotics and Industrial Automation

Industrial robotics has traditionally focused on repetitive, well-defined tasks in controlled environments such as assembly lines, where robots perform precise, repeatable motions far faster and more consistently than human workers. Advances in machine vision and sensor technology have expanded the range of tasks robots can perform, enabling them to identify and manipulate objects with variable position, orientation, or shape rather than requiring parts to be precisely positioned in advance. Collaborative robots, often called cobots, are designed to work safely alongside human workers in shared spaces, using force-limiting joints and sensors that stop or slow movement if a person comes too close, in contrast to traditional industrial robots that operate in caged, human-free zones. Beyond manufacturing, robotics is increasingly applied in logistics and warehousing, where autonomous mobile robots navigate dynamic environments to transport goods, and in agriculture, where robots can perform tasks such as selective harvesting that require distinguishing ripe from unripe produce.
