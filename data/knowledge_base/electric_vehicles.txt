Electric Vehicles

Electric vehicles use battery-powered electric motors instead of internal combustion engines, eliminating tailpipe emissions and reducing the number of moving mechanical parts that require maintenance. Battery cost has historically been the largest barrier to adoption, but prices per kilowatt-hour have fallen substantially over the past decade as manufacturing has scaled and chemistry has improved, narrowing the upfront price gap with conventional vehicles. Charging infrastructure remains a key constraint on adoption, particularly for drivers without access to home charging, which has driven investment in public fast-charging networks along highways and in urban areas. Battery range and charging speed continue to improve, though they still generally lag the refueling speed of gasoline vehicles for long-distance travel. The environmental benefit of electric vehicles depends significantly on the carbon intensity of the electricity grid used to charge them, meaning the climate case for EVs strengthens as grids incorporate more renewable generation.
