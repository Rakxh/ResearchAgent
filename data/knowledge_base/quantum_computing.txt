Quantum Computing

Quantum computing exploits properties of quantum mechanics, such as superposition and entanglement, to perform certain calculations far more efficiently than classical computers. Instead of bits that are strictly zero or one, quantum computers use qubits that can represent combinations of states simultaneously, enabling certain algorithms to explore many possible solutions in parallel. Promising application areas include cryptography, where quantum algorithms could eventually break widely used encryption schemes, and chemistry, where simulating molecular interactions is computationally expensive for classical machines but more natural for quantum systems. Current quantum hardware remains limited by noise and short coherence times, meaning qubits lose their quantum state quickly, which restricts the complexity of problems that can be solved reliably today. Researchers are pursuing error correction techniques and alternative qubit designs, including superconducting circuits and trapped ions, to build larger, more stable quantum processors.
